## Introduction

**Adi Shankaracharya** (à¤†à¤¦à¤¿ à¤¶à¤™à¥à¤•à¤°à¤¾à¤šà¤¾à¤°à¥à¤¯), also known as **Adi Shankara**, was an 8th-century Indian philosopher and theologian who systematized the philosophy of **Advaita Vedanta**. In his short lifespan of 32 years, he produced an extraordinary body of work that includes:

- **Bhashyas** (commentaries) on the foundational texts of Vedanta
- **Prakarana Granthas** (independent philosophical treatises)
- **Stotras** (devotional hymns)

His works emphasize the fundamental unity of the individual soul (**Atman**) with the universal consciousness (**Brahman**), stating: **"Brahma Satyam Jagat Mithya, Jivo Brahmaiva Na Aparah"** (Brahman alone is real, the world is illusory, the individual soul is none other than Brahman).

## Bhashyas (Commentaries)

Shankaracharya's commentaries (_bhashyas_) form the foundation of Advaita Vedanta. His most important work is the commentary on the **Prasthanatrayi** (Three Sources), the canonical texts of Vedanta.

### Prasthanatrayi Bhashyas

#### 1. **Brahma Sutra Bhashya** (à¤¬à¥à¤°à¤¹à¥à¤®à¤¸à¥‚à¤¤à¥à¤°à¤­à¤¾à¤·à¥à¤¯)

**Also known as**: Vedanta Sutra Bhashya, Shariraka Bhashya
**Original Text**: Brahma Sutras by Badarayana (555 aphorisms in 4 chapters)
**Shankara's Contribution**: Considered his masterpiece and magnum opus

**Content**:

- Systematic exposition of Vedantic philosophy
- Establishes Brahman as the non-dual ultimate reality
- Refutes alternative philosophical schools (Samkhya, Yoga, Buddhism, Jainism)
- Reconciles apparent contradictions in Upanishadic teachings

**Four Chapters**:

1. **Samanvaya** (Harmony) - Brahman is the source of the universe
2. **Avirodha** (Non-conflict) - Refutes objections to Vedanta
3. **Sadhana** (Means) - The path to realize Brahman
4. **Phala** (Fruit) - The nature of liberation (Moksha)

---

#### 2. **Bhagavad Gita Bhashya** (à¤­à¤—à¤µà¤¦à¥à¤—à¥€à¤¤à¤¾à¤­à¤¾à¤·à¥à¤¯)

**Original Text**: Bhagavad Gita (18 chapters, 700 verses)
**Significance**: Brought the Gita to prominence in Hindu philosophy

**Key Themes in Commentary**:

- **Karma Yoga** (path of action) as preparation for knowledge
- **Jnana Yoga** (path of knowledge) as the ultimate means to liberation
- **Bhakti Yoga** (path of devotion) as purification of mind
- The immortality of Atman
- Renunciation of fruits of action (_Nishkama Karma_)

**Famous Verses Commented**:

- Chapter 2, Verse 16: "The unreal has no existence; the real never ceases to be"
- Chapter 2, Verse 47: "You have a right to perform your duty, but not to the fruits of action"
- Chapter 15, Verse 7: "An eternal fragment of Myself becomes the living being"

### Other Bhashyas

#### 4. **Vishnu Sahasranama Bhashya**

- Commentary on the 1,000 names of Vishnu from Mahabharata
- Explains each name from Advaitic perspective

#### 5. **Sanatsujatiya Bhashya**

- Commentary on philosophical dialogue from Mahabharata
- Discourse between Sanatsujata and King Dhritarashtra

#### 6. **Lalita Trishati Bhashya**

- Commentary on 300 names of Goddess Lalita
- Devotional and philosophical interpretation

#### 7. **Hastamalakiya Bhashya**

- Commentary on verses by his disciple Hastamalaka
- Short work on self-realization

## Prakarana Granthas (Philosophical Treatises)

These are independent works that present Advaita philosophy in accessible formats. While authorship of some is debated by modern scholars, they are traditionally attributed to Shankara.

### Universally Accepted as Authentic

#### 1. **Upadeshasahasri** (à¤‰à¤ªà¤¦à¥‡à¤¶à¤¸à¤¾à¤¹à¤¸à¥à¤°à¥€)

_"A Thousand Teachings"_

**Format**: Prose (Gadya) and Verse (Padya) sections
**Content**:

- 18 chapters in verse (Padya)
- 3 chapters in prose (Gadya)
- Systematic instruction on Advaita Vedanta
- Direct teaching method for spiritual aspirants

**Key Teachings**:

- Method of self-inquiry (_Atma-vichara_)
- Distinction between Self and non-Self
- Prerequisites for spiritual knowledge
- The nature of ignorance (_Avidya_) and liberation (_Moksha_)

**Famous Verse**: "Brahman is the reality, the world is illusory, the individual soul is nothing but Brahman itself"

_"Crest-Jewel of Discrimination"_

**Length**: 580 verses
**Format**: Dialogue between Guru and disciple

**Content**:

- Step-by-step spiritual practice (_sadhana_)
- Discrimination between real (eternal) and unreal (temporal)
- Four-fold qualifications (_Sadhana Chatushtaya_)
- Analysis of three states (waking, dream, deep sleep)

**Key Concepts**:

1. **Viveka** - Discrimination
2. **Vairagya** - Dispassion
3. **Shatsampat** - Six virtues (tranquility, self-control, etc.)
4. **Mumukshutva** - Intense desire for liberation

**Famous Verses**:

- "Birth as a human being is rare, rarer still is the desire for liberation"
- "Brahman is the only truth, the world is unreal, and there is ultimately no difference between Brahman and individual self"

_"Self-Knowledge"_

**Length**: 68 verses
**Audience**: Beginners in Vedanta

- Methods to discriminate Self from non-Self

- Analogies to explain non-duality

**Famous Analogies**:

- Space in pot vs. infinite space (individual vs. universal consciousness)
- Wave and ocean (multiplicity in unity)

### Purpose of Stotras

Shankara's stotras serve multiple purposes:

1. **Purification of Mind** (_Chitta Shuddhi_): Devotion prepares the mind for knowledge
2. **Emotional Expression**: Channeling devotion to the divine
3. **Demonstration**: Showing that Advaita doesn't negate devotion
4. **Cultural Integration**: Making philosophy accessible through devotional practice
5. **Poetic Excellence**: Showcasing Sanskrit literary mastery

## Core Philosophical Concepts

### Advaita Vedanta: The Non-Dual Philosophy

#### The Central Teaching

**"Brahma Satyam Jagat Mithya, Jivo Brahmaiva Na Aparah"**
_Brahman alone is real, the world is illusory (appearance), the individual soul is not different from Brahman_

#### Key Concepts

**1. Brahman**

- The ultimate, infinite, unchanging reality
- **Nirguna Brahman**: Without attributes, formless
- **Saguna Brahman**: With attributes, personal God (Ishvara)
- Existence-Consciousness-Bliss (Sat-Chit-Ananda)

**2. Atman**

- The individual Self/Soul
- Identical with Brahman
- Pure consciousness, eternal, unchanging

**3. Maya**

- Cosmic illusion/appearance
- Power of Brahman that projects the universe
- Neither real nor unreal (_anirvachaniya_)
- Causes the appearance of multiplicity

**4. Avidya (Ignorance)**

- Individual ignorance
- Root cause of suffering
- Removed only by knowledge (_Jnana_)

**5. Moksha (Liberation)**

- Realization of one's true nature as Brahman
- Freedom from cycle of birth and death
- Not a state to be achieved but reality to be realized
- Can be attained in this life (_Jivanmukti_)

#### The Four Mahavakyas (Great Sentences)

These aphorisms from the Upanishads encapsulate Advaita:

1. **"Prajnanam Brahma"** (à¤ªà¥à¤°à¤œà¥à¤à¤¾à¤¨à¤‚ à¤¬à¥à¤°à¤¹à¥à¤®)
   - "Consciousness is Brahman"
   - From Aitareya Upanishad (Rig Veda)

2. **"Tat Tvam Asi"** (à¤¤à¤¤à¥ à¤¤à¥à¤µà¤®à¥ à¤…à¤¸à¤¿)
   - "That Thou Art"
   - From Chandogya Upanishad (Sama Veda)
   - Most famous Mahavakya

3. **"Ayam Atma Brahma"** (à¤…à¤¯à¤®à¥ à¤†à¤¤à¥à¤®à¤¾ à¤¬à¥à¤°à¤¹à¥à¤®)
   - "This Self is Brahman"
   - From Mandukya Upanishad (Atharva Veda)

4. **"Aham Brahmasmi"** (à¤…à¤¹à¤‚ à¤¬à¥à¤°à¤¹à¥à¤®à¤¾à¤¸à¥à¤®à¤¿)
   - "I am Brahman"
   - From Brihadaranyaka Upanishad (Yajur Veda)

#### Three Levels of Reality

1. **Paramarthika Satta** (Absolute Reality)
   - Brahman alone
   - Unchanging, eternal

2. **Vyavaharika Satta** (Empirical Reality)
   - The world of daily experience
   - Relatively real, governed by laws

3. **Pratibhasika Satta** (Apparent Reality)
   - Illusions, dreams, errors
   - Subjectively real, objectively false

#### Paths to Liberation

While knowledge (_Jnana_) is the direct means, Shankara acknowledges preparatory paths:

1. **Karma Yoga**: Selfless action to purify the mind
2. **Bhakti Yoga**: Devotion to prepare the heart
3. **Jnana Yoga**: Direct knowledge of the Self (ultimate path)
4. **Raja Yoga**: Meditation and mental discipline

#### Qualifications for Knowledge (_Sadhana Chatushtaya_)

1. **Viveka**: Discrimination between eternal and temporal
2. **Vairagya**: Dispassion toward worldly pleasures
3. **Shatsampat**: Six virtues
   - Shama (tranquility)
   - Dama (self-control)
   - Uparati (withdrawal)
   - Titiksha (forbearance)
   - Shraddha (faith)
   - Samadhana (concentration)
4. **Mumukshutva**: Intense desire for liberation

## The Four Mathas

Shankara established four monastic institutions (_mathas_) across India to preserve and propagate Vedantic teachings:

| Matha                        | Location               | Direction | Veda         | Mahavakya          | Current Head Title            |
| ---------------------------- | ---------------------- | --------- | ------------ | ------------------ | ----------------------------- |
| **Sringeri Sharada Peetham** | Sringeri, Karnataka    | South     | Yajur Veda   | "Aham Brahmasmi"   | Jagadguru                     |
| **Dwaraka Peetham**          | Dwarka, Gujarat        | West      | Sama Veda    | "Tat Tvam Asi"     | Shankaracharya of Dwarka      |
| **Jyotir Math**              | Joshimath, Uttarakhand | North     | Atharva Veda | "Ayam Atma Brahma" | Shankaracharya of Jyotir Math |
| **Govardhana Peetham**       | Puri, Odisha           | East      | Rig Veda     | "Prajnanam Brahma" | Shankaracharya of Puri        |

Each matha continues the tradition of Vedic scholarship and spiritual guidance.

## Legacy and Influence

### Impact on Hinduism

1. **Unified Hindu Thought**: Consolidated various philosophical currents
2. **Debate and Reform**: Defeated Buddhist and other schools in theological debates
3. **Revival**: Revitalized Vedic traditions across India
4. **Monastic Tradition**: Established Dashanami Sampradaya (10 orders of monks)
5. **Temple Reforms**: Reorganized temple worship practices

### Philosophical Contributions

1. **Commentarial Tradition**: Set standard for Sanskrit commentary (_bhashya_)
2. **Non-Dualism**: Firmly established Advaita as a major school
3. **Integration**: Harmonized Upanishads, Brahma Sutras, and Bhagavad Gita
4. **Methodology**: Combined reason (_yukti_) with scripture (_shruti_)

### Cultural Influence

1. **Sanskrit Poetry**: Stotras remain popular in Hindu devotional practice
2. **Intellectual Tradition**: Influenced later philosophers (Vidyaranya, Sadananda, etc.)
3. **Pan-Indian Presence**: Through his four mathas
4. **Art and Music**: Stotras set to music (Carnatic and Hindustani)

### Modern Relevance

1. **Swami Vivekananda**: Propagated Advaita Vedanta to the West
2. **Ramana Maharshi**: Taught self-inquiry based on Advaitic principles
3. **Academic Study**: Subject of extensive scholarly research
4. **Interfaith Dialogue**: Advaita's universal message appeals globally

## Conclusion

Adi Shankaracharya's works represent one of the most comprehensive philosophical systems in world thought. His genius lay in:

1. **Synthesis**: Harmonizing diverse Upanishadic teachings
2. **Clarity**: Making complex philosophy accessible
3. **Balance**: Integrating knowledge (_jnana_) and devotion (_bhakti_)
4. **Practicality**: Providing both intellectual understanding and practical guidance
5. **Universality**: Teaching the oneness of all existence
